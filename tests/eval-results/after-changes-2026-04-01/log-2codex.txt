## Decision

The council’s consensus is that the startup should not build full in-house payment processing infrastructure now and should continue using Stripe as its primary processor. Instead, it should immediately renegotiate Stripe pricing, run a 90-day discovery/commercial program, and only consider a narrowly scoped hybrid path if a specific transaction corridor still shows at least `>=100 bps` net savings after Stripe repricing, headcount, compliance/ops overhead, and conservative auth-rate risk. Reversible “no-regrets” work such as corridor-level cost instrumentation, reconciliation data modeling, and portability/vendor-readiness prep is acceptable; owning production routing, settlement, or processor logic is not.

## Key Disagreements

| # | What Was Debated | Resolution | Who Had the Strongest Case & Why |
|---|-----------------|------------|----------------------------------|
| 1 | Full in-house payments infrastructure vs staying on Stripe | Full in-house ownership was rejected; Stripe remains the primary processor. | Skeptic had the strongest case because they showed most of the current `~300 bps` is not recoverable, and highlighted PCI, sponsor-bank, money-transmission, dispute, and operational risks that a 20-engineer Series A team is poorly positioned to absorb. |
| 2 | Whether the company should start building internal payments capabilities now | Only reversible prep work should start now; no production routing, settlement, or processor abstraction should go live until economics are proven. | Advocate had the strongest case on timing because they framed early prep as option value: waiting until volume reaches `~$150M` would increase lock-in and weaken negotiating leverage. |
| 3 | What economic threshold justifies any hybrid move away from Stripe | The bar is high: after Stripe IC+/custom pricing and external quotes, at least one corridor must show `>=100 bps` net savings with a credible 12-month payback. | Skeptic had the strongest case because they shifted the conversation from gross fee savings to true net savings after team cost, compliance/ops burden, and migration/auth regression risk. |

## Unresolved

- The company still does not know how much of the current `~$1.5M/year` Stripe spend is recoverable after custom pricing or IC+.
- It remains unproven whether any single high-volume corridor can clear the `>=100 bps` net-savings hurdle.
- PCI scope, token portability limits, refund/dispute handling, and authorization-rate regression remain major migration risks even in a hybrid model.
- The exact boundary between acceptable “no-regrets” prep work and scope-expanding production infrastructure should be explicitly documented before engineering starts.

## Confidence: High
Both agents converged on the core answer: reject full in-house processing, keep Stripe, renegotiate now, and gate any hybrid buildout behind hard corridor-level economics and compliance discipline.

## Decision

The council converged on a staged middle path: do not spend 12-18 months chasing a headline `95% accuracy` target before engaging FDA, but also do not pursue broad deployment now based only on `87% accuracy`. The company should start FDA pre-sub immediately, lock a minimum viable commercial workflow, and run a fast prospective multi-site study; they should file for clearance and launch a tightly scoped human-in-the-loop product only if that study shows strong `sensitivity`, `specificity`, `imageability`, subgroup robustness, and safe coverage at the intended operating point.

## Key Disagreements

| # | What Was Debated | Resolution | Who Had the Strongest Case & Why |
|---|-----------------|------------|----------------------------------|
| 1 | Whether `87% accuracy` matching average radiologist performance is enough to move forward | `87% accuracy` alone is not decision-grade for DR screening; the relevant bar is `sensitivity`, `specificity`, `imageability`, subgroup performance, and conservative handling of ungradable images on the actual workflow | Skeptic had the strongest case because they showed that FDA-cleared comparators are evaluated on clinically specific metrics, not headline accuracy |
| 2 | Whether the company should deploy now or wait to reach `95% accuracy` | Neither extreme won; the group rejected both immediate broad deployment and waiting for `95%` as a gating target | Advocate had the strongest case against waiting because the runway and value-of-information argument made a long offline optimization cycle look strategically inferior |
| 3 | Whether human-in-the-loop meaningfully reduces regulatory and safety risk | HITL helps only if the team workflow is prospectively validated; it is not a blanket exemption or sufficient mitigation by itself | Skeptic had the strongest case because they tied HITL limits to FDA CDS/device principles and real workflow failure modes |
| 4 | Whether engaging FDA now risks freezing the wrong product too early | The company should avoid over-freezing a narrow lab setup, but should still lock a minimum viable commercial workflow soon enough to generate decision-grade evidence | Skeptic had the strongest case because the “product trap” risk was concrete and changed the advocate’s earlier “file now” stance |
| 5 | What the best near-term strategy is under 18 months of runway | Immediate FDA pre-sub plus a 3-6 month prospective multi-site study is the preferred path because it generates financing, partnership, and regulatory signal faster than chasing benchmark gains | Advocate had the strongest case because they reframed the problem as time-to-evidence and survival, not just model optimization |

## Unresolved

- The current model’s actual `sensitivity`, `specificity`, `imageability`, and subgroup performance are still unknown from the discussion record.
- It remains unresolved whether safe performance is achievable at a commercially viable coverage rate once low-confidence and ungradable cases are abstained or routed out.
- A too-narrow locked workflow could win a study but produce a product that is hard to scale commercially.
- A weak prospective readout could consume runway without yielding clearance, financing leverage, or a usable product.
- Real-world degradation across sites, cameras, patient populations, and referral workflows remains a material risk even if internal validation looks acceptable.

## Confidence: High
Both agents converged by Round 3 on the same core recommendation: reject `95% accuracy` as the gate, generate prospective workflow-level evidence now, and make filing contingent on those results rather than on benchmark accuracy alone.

## Decision

They should not commit to migrating all 35 repos into a monorepo up front. The consensus is to run a real, time-boxed monorepo pilot for the 8-12 most tightly coupled, same-stack repos, because the current pattern of routine 3-5 repo changes is strong evidence that the existing source boundaries no longer match how the system evolves. If the pilot improves coordination without degrading CI and merge flow, they can expand or stop at a hybrid model.

## Key Disagreements

| # | What Was Debated | Resolution | Who Had the Strongest Case & Why |
|---|-----------------|------------|----------------------------------|
| 1 | Whether the company’s pain is fundamentally caused by having many repos, or by badly aligned change boundaries | The main signal is not repo count but the fact that ordinary work already requires 3-5 coordinated PRs, version bumps, and sequencing across repos | Advocate, because the change-topology argument best explained why the current structure is imposing recurring coordination tax |
| 2 | Whether the answer should be a full monorepo migration or staying polyrepo with better tooling | Neither extreme won; the agreed path is an incremental pilot for the highest-coupling slice, while leaving the rest separate for now | Skeptic, because the strongest caution was that a blanket 35-repo migration is unjustified without proving the benefits on the same-stack cluster first |
| 3 | Whether monorepo benefits outweigh the platform cost and operational risk | Only if the pilot includes the real operating model: affected CI, remote caching, module boundaries, CODEOWNERS, and independent deploys | Skeptic, because the “platform tax” argument clarified that a file move without supporting systems would produce misleading results |
| 4 | Whether the migration is a one-way strategic commitment | It should be treated as a reversible experiment with explicit go/no-go thresholds and a possible hybrid end state | Advocate, because reframing the move as a bounded, reversible bet materially reduced the perceived migration risk |

## Unresolved

- Which exact 8-12 repos account for most multi-repo changes, and whether they truly share the same language/build stack
- Whether leadership will fund named platform ownership, likely 2-4 engineers, for CI, caching, boundaries, and release automation during the pilot
- The exact success thresholds for PR lead time, CI p95 duration, merge queue time, revert rate, and reduction in multi-repo changes
- The risk that shared-library changes still fan out widely and create CI or merge bottlenecks even inside a monorepo
- The possibility that some repos should remain separate because of heterogeneous tooling, compliance, access-control, or release-cadence differences

## Confidence: High
Both agents converged on the same recommendation by the end: a bounded monorepo pilot for the tightly coupled slice, with disagreement remaining mainly on execution discipline rather than direction.
